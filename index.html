⚠ BEFORE YOU SEARCH — READ THIS

LLMs feel like a smarter way to shop — but most are trained on the same affiliate blogs, SEO listicles, and sponsored content that pollutes Google. The result looks authoritative but is often just well-marketed consensus.

This tool builds a prompt that forces your LLM to stress-test conventional wisdom rather than confirm it — turning "what's the best X?" into "what does the owner community actually complain about?" Every prompt is adversarial by design. That's where the real signal is.

► What are GEO, dead web, and astroturfing?
GEO (Generative Engine Optimization) Brands now write content designed to influence LLM training data — not human readers. LLMs end up recommending products that were best at describing themselves to an AI, not products that are actually best.
Dead web The internet is dominated by low-effort affiliate content — "Top 10 Toasters of 2025!" written by people who never touched the products. LLM training data is saturated with it. "Consensus" often just means successful marketing.
Astroturfed Reddit Brands hire agencies to create aged accounts and post convincing product recommendations in subreddits. People trusted Reddit because it felt like real humans — which is exactly why it became a target.
Affiliate flagging Sites that earn commissions on sales have a financial incentive to recommend products regardless of quality. A good LLM response identifies these sources so you can weigh them accordingly.

⚠ Which LLM to use: Claude and ChatGPT both work well. Use a thinking or reasoning mode if your LLM offers one — it produces noticeably more thorough, self-critical results. Avoid fast or quick modes. Note on Gemini: Google may inject paid Shopping links into responses regardless of your instructions. Gemini also has a known pattern of generating convincing-looking fake bibliographies — plausible source descriptions constructed after the fact to fit claims already made, rather than sources actually consulted. Check any citations before trusting them.
📖 HOW TO READ YOUR RESULTS
✅ GREEN FLAGS — it did the work
Cites specific forum threads or communities, not just "reviews say"
Names failure modes and which community reported them
Has an explicit "NOT recommending" section
Flags affiliate sources inline
Top pick differs from the universal consensus
Distinguishes product variants (e.g. Gaggia Evo Pro vs Classic Pro)
Includes total cost of ownership, not just sticker price
Ends with genuine uncertainty — admits what it doesn't know
⛔ RED FLAGS — generic consensus answer
First recommendation matches every other site's
No citations, or vague ones ("according to experts")
No failure modes, or one sentence buried at the end
Ends with an upsell ("want me to find accessories?")
Confident throughout — no uncertainty anywhere
Wirecutter / Tom's Guide cited without affiliate flag
Commercial shopping links embedded in the response
The same 3–4 machines that appear everywhere
Reddit cited but no thread title or URL — Reddit is largely inaccessible to LLMs and unverifiable Reddit citations may be fabricated
Numbered footnotes ([Source 1.2]) with no publication name or URL — these are citation formatting, not actual citations
Bibliography provided only after you ask — a real citation is given at the time of the claim, not constructed afterwards to fit it
⚠ Hardest things to detect: The LLM may find real forum threads but cherry-pick positive ones. "r/espresso recommends X" without finding the "please stop recommending X" thread is a half-answer. If a recommendation feels too clean, ask: "Find the most-upvoted criticism of [product] on Reddit."

Reddit is largely inaccessible to LLMs — most crawlers are blocked. An LLM that confidently cites specific subreddit threads without a verifiable URL or thread title may be fabricating those citations. Accurate-sounding facts wrapped in fake sourcing are harder to catch than obvious hallucinations.
